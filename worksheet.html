Part A · Image Classifier demo: Image Classifier

A convolutional network (ResNet-50) sorts a photo into one of 1,000 everyday labels, as a ranked list of probabilities. Open the demo, press Classify this image, and try each picture.

  1. Record the confident hits. Classify each and write the model's top label and its percentage.

    ImageTop labelConfidence %
    Hot-air balloon
    Airliner
    Geyser

  2. Now the hard ones. Classify these and record what it guessed instead, and how sure it was.

    ImageTop label (its best guess)Confidence %
    Aurora
    Storm cloud
    Earth from space
    Snow-covered volcano

  3. Why the difference? Three of those four have no matching label in the model's 1,000 words at all. The volcano does have a "volcano" label, yet the model still misses it. Why might that be?

  4. Break it slowly. Take a picture it labels confidently and drag Blur up one step at a time. How many steps before the top label changes? What does it become?

  5. Colour or shape? On a picture it recognises, turn on Greyscale. Does the guess survive? What does that tell you about what the network relies on?

  6. Big idea. This model was trained on ordinary web photos, not atmospheric science. In one sentence: what would you have to do to make it recognise auroras or cloud types reliably?

Part B · Build a Filter by Hand demo: Build a Filter by Hand

A CNN is built from one operation repeated: slide a small grid of numbers (a filter) over the image, multiply and add. Open the demo and edit the nine numbers to see what each filter detects.

  1. Identity. Load the Identity preset (a 1 in the centre, 0s around it). The output looks like the input, why? Now change one corner 0 to a 1 and run. What happens to the picture?

  2. Build an edge detector. Type this filter in by hand, then run it. Describe the output image in a few words.

    -1-1-1-18-1-1-1-1

  3. Direction matters. Compare Sobel horizontal and Sobel vertical. What is different about the two feature maps, and why?

  4. The divisor. Load the Blur (box) preset. Its nine weights are all 1, so they add up to 9, and it divides by 9. In the JavaScript, change sum / divisor to just sum and run. What happens to the image, and why?

  5. Connect it up. You chose these nine numbers by hand. Where does a real CNN's filters come from instead? (One sentence.)

Words to know

Pixel
one dot of the image; the network only ever sees a grid of these as numbers.
Filter / kernel
a small grid of numbers slid over the image to detect a pattern.
Convolution
the sliding-multiply-add operation that applies a filter across the whole image.
Feature map
the output of one filter: an image that is bright where the pattern was found.
CNN (convolutional neural network)
many layers of learned filters stacked together.
Probability distribution
the model's answer is a ranked spread across all labels that adds up to 100%, not a single answer.
Confidence
the percentage on the top label; high does not always mean correct.

Teacher answer key

Print pages 1–2 for students; keep this page. Exact labels/percentages vary by browser and model version, these are the ideas to look for.

  1. Part A, Q1 (hits). Balloon → "balloon"; airliner → "airliner"; geyser → "geyser", all typically high confidence. The point: it works well on ordinary objects it was trained on.
  2. Part A, Q2 (misses). Aurora, cloud and Earth have no matching label, so it returns something unrelated (often at low or oddly-high confidence). The volcano usually comes back as something like a snowy mountain, an alp, or an unrelated object.
  3. Part A, Q3 (why). ImageNet has no "aurora", "cloud" or "planet" label, the model can only choose among its 1,000 words, so it must pick the nearest wrong one. The volcano has a label but this photo (snow-covered, blown-out crater) looks nothing like the tidy cones the model learned from, so it still misses. Lesson: a general model has not met your domain; matching a label is not enough, the image must resemble the training data.
  4. Part A, Q4 (blur). Answers vary; the key observation is that confidence falls and the ranking reshuffles as detail is destroyed, eventually flipping to a different label. The network reads texture and edges, blur removes them.
  5. Part A, Q5 (greyscale). Usually the guess survives, showing the network leans more on shape and texture than on colour.
  6. Part A, Q6 (big idea). Collect labelled examples from the domain (auroras, cloud types) and train a model on them, or fine-tune this one. Same machinery, different training data and labels.
  7. Part B, Q1 (identity). The centre weight 1 copies each pixel unchanged, everything else is multiplied by 0. Adding a 1 in a corner adds a shifted copy of the image (a faint ghost / doubling in that direction).
  8. Part B, Q2 (edge). Bright outlines on a dark background, edges and boundaries light up; flat areas go dark because the weights sum to zero.
  9. Part B, Q3 (Sobel). One highlights horizontal edges, the other vertical, because each filter's positive and negative weights are arranged along a different axis. A CNN keeps many filters to catch every orientation.
  10. Part B, Q4 (divisor). Without dividing, the nine added pixels make the image roughly nine times too bright, so it washes out to white. The divisor keeps the average brightness the same.
  11. Part B, Q5 (connect). A CNN starts with random numbers and learns its filter weights from many labelled examples, it discovers useful detectors (like edges) on its own rather than being told them.
