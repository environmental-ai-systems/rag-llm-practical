Go deeper: how real systems decide where to cut

This demo uses the bluntest method there is, a fixed-size sliding window that cuts every N units regardless of meaning, which is exactly why it will happily slice a sentence in half. It's the easiest to reason about, so it's a good place to start.

Structure-aware splitting does better by cutting on natural boundaries, paragraphs, sentences, or Markdown headings, so each chunk is a coherent unit. A recursive splitter tries the largest boundary first (paragraphs), then falls back to smaller ones (sentences, then words) only when a piece is still too long.

Semantic chunking goes further still: it embeds each sentence and starts a new chunk wherever the topic shifts. Slower to build, but the boundaries land where the meaning actually changes.

The size trade-off is the real lesson. Small chunks retrieve precisely (a hit points at the exact relevant sentence) but each carries little surrounding context, and one answer can end up scattered across several. Large chunks carry more context but retrieve less precisely (you drag in unrelated text) and dilute the embedding, because a single vector now has to stand for many ideas at once.

Overlap is cheap insurance that an idea straddling a boundary survives whole in at least one chunk, paid for in duplicated text and extra vectors to store and search. A common starting point is a few hundred tokens per chunk with roughly 10 to 20% overlap, then tuned against your own documents and the questions people actually ask. There is no universal best setting.

Peek inside: what actually is a token?

A token isn't a word and it isn't a letter, it's a subword piece from a fixed vocabulary of about 100,000 fragments (GPT-4 uses the set called cl100k). Common words are usually a single token; rarer or longer words get split into several; and the leading space, capital letters, and punctuation all count. Two reasons this matters: a model's context window and its price are both measured in tokens, not words, and chunking by tokens is how real systems make sure a chunk actually fits. Go deeper: why the token count matters

Everything shares one budget. A model reads and writes in tokens, and its context window is a fixed number of them. The instructions, the chunks you retrieved, the question, and the model's own answer all have to fit inside that budget at once. Chunking exists largely so you can spend it well.

Cost is measured the same way. Model pricing is per token, so chunk size multiplied by how many chunks you retrieve is a direct lever on both speed and bill.

Rough conversions for English prose: about 4 characters, or 0.75 words, per token. Code, long numbers, URLs, and non-English scripts usually cost more tokens for the same amount of text, worth remembering if your documents aren't plain English.

What the inspector reveals that a word count hides: a leading space belongs to the token, so the same word with and without a space in front tokenises differently; capitalisation changes the tokens; and a rare or misspelled word can shatter into several pieces.

3

Retrieve

Ask a question. The system scores every chunk for relevance and brings back the best matches. This is the text that would be handed to the LLM to answer from.

Keyword match (TF-IDF) ranks chunks by the words they share with your question. Simple and transparent, but blind to meaning: ask in different words and it can miss the answer.

Results will appear here.

The number on each result is its relevance score, higher means a closer match, and the bar draws that score relative to the top hit. What the number actually is:

Go deeper: what exactly is that score?

In keyword mode the score is a cosine similarity between two bags of words: your question and the chunk. Each word is weighted up if it's rare across the chunks (so it's distinctive, like "asthma") and down if it's common (like "the", which is dropped entirely). The score runs from 0, no important words in common, to 1, the same weighted words in the same proportions. Sharing one rare, on-topic word moves it far more than sharing several everyday ones. This weighting scheme is called TF-IDF.

In semantic mode it's the same cosine idea, but comparing the 384-number meaning vectors instead of word counts. Worth knowing: even an excellent semantic match usually scores well below 1, often 0.3 to 0.6. Each embedding captures many facets of a passage at once, its topic, but also its tone, its particular words, its structure, and two passages rarely line up on every one, so a perfect 1.0 is rare. Read the ranking and the gaps between scores, rather than the absolute figure.

The bar is just a picture of the number. Each result's bar is drawn as a proportion of the top hit, so the best match is always full. It shows relative confidence within this one search, it isn't an absolute grade, and you can't compare a bar here against a bar from a different question.

Stage 4's repository search reuses the very same keyword score, measured over each document's title and abstract.

Go deeper: what a semantic embedding actually is

An embedding turns a piece of text into a list of numbers, here 384 of them, placed so that texts with similar meaning sit near each other, even when they share no words. The model learned those positions from enormous amounts of text: "cough" and "respiratory symptom" land close together because they tend to appear in similar contexts.

Closeness is measured by cosine similarity, essentially the angle between two vectors. Because the vectors here are normalised, that's simply their dot product, and a score of 1.0 means they point in exactly the same direction.

This is why semantic search can find the right passage when the wording is completely different from the question. It's also why it can occasionally return something topically close but not actually an answer, it matches meaning, not truth. That failure mode, confidently handing back something related but wrong, is worth watching out for.

Search a repository

Everything so far searched inside one document. But real systems search across a whole repository, many documents, each carrying structured metadata: who wrote it, when, what kind of thing it is, what it's about. There are two very different ways to narrow it down, and telling them apart is the whole point of this stage.
