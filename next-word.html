What a language model is really doing

Guess the next token

Type a few words and watch a real, if tiny, language model predict what comes next: not one answer, but a spread of probabilities over its whole vocabulary. Keep going and it writes, one token at a time. It all runs in your browser; nothing you type leaves this tab.

1

The prompt

A language model only ever sees a sequence of tokens, and it answers one question: what token is likely to come next? Give it a few words to start from.

Those coloured boxes are how the model sees your text, subword tokens, not whole words. ␣ marks a leading space.

2

Predict, then generate

Here's the model's answer: the most likely next tokens, each with a probability. Pick one yourself, or let the model choose, and it becomes part of the text. Then the same question repeats. That loop is generation.

Add most likely always takes the top-ranked token, the safe, predictable choice (this is called greedy). Add a sample instead picks one at random, weighted by the probabilities, so likelier tokens are chosen more often but surprises can slip in; this is what real text generation does, and it's what the temperature dial controls. Auto-write just repeats "Add a sample" fifteen times.

Go deeper: where those percentages come from

For every position, the model produces one raw score, a logit, for each of the roughly 50,000 tokens it knows. On their own those numbers don't mean much; they can be any size, positive or negative.

A step called softmax squashes the whole list into probabilities: it exponentiates each score and divides by the total, so they all land between 0 and 1 and add up to exactly 1. That's the distribution you see, the bars are just its top ten, out of tens of thousands.

Temperature is a dial applied before softmax. Turn it down and the gap between scores is exaggerated, so the top token dominates and output turns predictable. Turn it up and the scores flatten toward each other, so unlikely tokens get a real chance and the text gets more surprising, and less reliable.

To actually write, the model picks one token from this distribution, the single most likely (greedy), or a random sample weighted by probability, sticks it on the end, and runs the whole thing again. Repeating that is called autoregressive generation.

Go deeper: why it repeats itself

Feed it "The weather today is" and you may get something like "a bit of a surprise. The weather is a bit of a surprise. The weather is…" It loops. That is expected, and worth understanding.

The model answers one question at a time: what token is most likely next? Nothing in it remembers "I already said that", and nothing rewards it for being fresh. With Add most likely (or a low temperature) it takes the safest token every single step. The catch: once it drifts into a phrase it finds probable, the safest continuation is the token that keeps that phrase going, which leads straight back into the same phrase. It's a self-reinforcing rut, and a tiny, old model like distilGPT-2 falls into it easily.

You can feel the mechanism by breaking the loop two ways. Raise the temperature and switch to Add a sample: now lower-ranked tokens get a real chance, so it occasionally steps off the well-worn path (at the cost of coherence). Real systems go further, adding a repetition penalty that deliberately lowers the score of tokens used recently, plus top-k / top-p sampling that trims the long tail of junk options. distilGPT-2 here has none of that, so you are seeing raw next-token prediction with the guard-rails off.

Go deeper: why it can sound confident and still be wrong

Notice the model is only ever predicting what's likely to come next, based on patterns in the text it was trained on. It has no notion of what's true, no memory of a specific source, and no way to look anything up. Fluent and correct are two different things.

The one here is distilGPT-2 (small, old, and trained on a slice of the 2019 web), so its mistakes are easy to see, which is exactly why it's useful for a demo. Bigger modern models are far more fluent, but the underlying move is identical: predict a plausible next token. That's the root of what people call hallucination.

So how do you get a model to answer from your documents rather than its vague memory of the internet? You fetch the right text and hand it to the model to work from. That's retrieval, and it's the subject of the next demo.

  1. The temperature dial. Set temperature to 0.2 and hit Auto-write, watch it turn safe and repetitive, often looping. Push it to 1.4 and try again, wilder, and less coherent. Low temperature is predictable; high is creative but unstable.
  2. Context is everything. Type "The capital of France is" and note the top token. Change France to Japan and predict again. Same model, different context, the only thing it has to go on is the tokens in front of it.
  3. Sure vs guessing. Find a prompt where the top token is above 80% (the model is almost certain) and one where the best is under 20% (it's basically guessing). What kind of text makes it confident?
  4. The gap retrieval fills. Start it with "PM2.5 harms the lungs because" and let it write. It produces something fluent that may or may not be right, because it's predicting plausible words, not looking anything up. Hold that thought for the retrieval demo.
