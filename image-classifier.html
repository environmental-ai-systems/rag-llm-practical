What a neural network sees in a picture

Show a machine a photo,
watch it guess

Hand a real image-recognition network (a convolutional neural network) a picture and it does one thing: it turns the pixels into a spread of probabilities over the 1,000 everyday labels it was trained on. Not one answer, a ranked guess. These photos mix a few everyday objects the network knows well with atmospheric-science scenes it was never trained on, so watch closely: some it nails, others it simply has no word for. Change the picture and watch the guess move. It all runs in your browser; nothing you upload leaves this tab.

  1. Hits and misses, you decide. Classify all six and sort them yourself. ImageNet does have a "volcano", "geyser" and "balloon" label, while aurora, cloud and "planet" simply aren't among its 1,000 words. But having the label is no guarantee: this volcano is snow-covered with a blown-out crater, nothing like the neat cones the model learned from, so see whether it finds "volcano" at all or reaches for something unrelated. That gap, between what a thing is and what a general model was trained to see, is the whole point.
  2. Break it slowly. Pick a picture it does label confidently, then drag Blur up one step at a time. Watch the top label's percentage fall and the runners-up climb. How blurry can it get before the answer flips?
  3. Colour vs shape. On a picture it recognises, turn on Greyscale. Does the guess survive? CNNs lean heavily on shape and texture, often more than colour, see if that holds.
  4. Upside down. Rotate a picture 180°. We still read it instantly; the network was trained on upright photos, so its confidence usually drops. Why might that be?
Go deeper: what the CNN is actually doing

The picture is first squashed to a fixed 224×224 grid of pixels, three numbers per pixel for red, green and blue. That grid of raw numbers is all the network ever gets, no labels, no caption, no context.

A convolutional neural network slides small filters across the image, each one a little pattern-detector. The earliest layers fire on edges and blobs of colour; stack more layers and they respond to corners, then textures, then whole parts, an eye, a wheel, a petal. A classic one, ResNet-50, is 50 layers deep (a newer CNN like ConvNeXt is more accurate; you can switch models below Stage 3). Nobody hand-wrote those filters; they were learned from about 1.2 million labelled photos (the ImageNet dataset).

At the very end the network produces one raw score, a logit, for each of its 1,000 possible labels. A step called softmax turns those scores into probabilities that add up to 100%. That is the ranked list you see, and it is why the model always answers something: even a picture of nothing it knows still gets a full distribution across those thousand words.

This is exactly why the atmospheric photos are revealing. ImageNet was built from everyday web images, so it has a "volcano" and a "geyser" but nothing for an aurora, a cloud type or a satellite view of Earth. To recognise those you would fine-tune this same network, or train a new one, on labelled images from your own field. The architecture stays the same; only the training data and the set of labels change.

Two things worth taking into the afternoon. First, the network has no idea what it doesn't know, its vocabulary is exactly those 1,000 labels and it cannot say "none of these". Second, it is only ever reporting what's statistically likely given the pixels and its training, which is exactly the move a language model makes with words. Confident and correct are not the same thing, here or there.
